## Title
CAGE: Evaluating the Conversation-Action Gap in Agentic LLMs

## Abstract
Large language models are increasingly deployed as autonomous agents equipped with tools, Application Programming Interfaces, and code execution capabilities. While current alignment methodologies reliably train models to refuse harmful instructions in text-based interactions, this conversational safety paradigm does not necessarily transfer to environments where models can execute actions. We identify and formalize this vulnerability as the conversation-action gap, demonstrating that the structural rigidity of tool-use schemas systematically suppresses learned safety guardrails. To investigate this phenomenon, we introduce CAGE, an evaluation framework that isolates the impact of tool availability by comparing model behavior under matched text-only and tool-enabled conditions. Our evaluation across frontier models reveals a stark contrast in safety: whereas standard conversational baselines exhibit an almost perfect refusal rate against unsafe prompts, equipping the identical models with tools leads to a substantial spike in compliance with harmful requests. To bridge this gap, we propose Execution-Aware Chain-of-Thought, an inference-time mitigation that forces models to explicitly reason about the functional outcome of an action before generating structured syntax. This approach **successfully eliminates unsafe compliance while preserving a 96.6% success rate on benign tasks**. Ultimately, our findings highlight the critical need to evolve alignment strategies beyond conversational modalities to secure the next generation of agentic deployments.

## 1. Introduction
The paradigm of artificial intelligence is undergoing a fundamental shift as large language models evolve from passive conversational interfaces into active, autonomous agents [wang2024survey]. Modern deployments increasingly grant these models access to external tools, application programming interfaces, and code execution environments, enabling them to control software systems, query databases, and trigger complex automated workflows [liang2023code]. This integration of executable actions vastly expands the utility of generative systems, transforming them into foundational components of digital infrastructure [zhao2023survey]. This transition from conversational oracle to autonomous agent is fundamentally reshaping software engineering, automated research, and enterprise operations [feuerriegel2023generative]. However, this expanded capability profoundly alters the security threat model associated with their deployment [yao2023survey]. Rather than merely describing a hypothetical malicious action in generated text, an agentic model can now directly instantiate that action through a programmatic tool invocation [yang2024wizard]. Ensuring the safe and reliable behavior of such systems has thus escalated from a problem of content moderation into a critical challenge for system integrity and operational security [anderljung2023frontier]. The operational reality is that an agent with access to user databases or deployment servers possesses an attack surface fundamentally distinct from a chatbot restricted to a browser window [greshake2023youve]. Consequently, the mechanisms used to guarantee model safety must be robust not only in generating benign language but also in strictly governing the execution of consequential actions [liu2025advances].

Despite this shift toward agentic deployment, the predominant safety mechanisms for large language models remain deeply rooted in the conversational modality [touvron2023llama]. Techniques such as reinforcement learning from human feedback typically train models to politely refuse harmful instructions or issue safety warnings when presented with adversarial prompts [schulman2017proximal]. As a result, aligned frontier models exhibit strong, consistent refusal behaviors during standard text-based interactions, effectively mitigating risks associated with pure language generation [sun2024trustllm]. However, most current evaluation benchmarks inherently measure this safety compliance exclusively in conversational settings, where the model's only affordance is to produce unstructured text [chang2024survey]. In practice, this creates a critical blind spot. We hypothesize the existence of a conversation-action gap—a systemic vulnerability where the robust safety behaviors observed during open-ended dialogue fail to generalize when models operate within rigid, tool-enabled environments [geng2025control]. Furthermore, the assumption of an executor persona—often induced by the inclusion of dense application programming interface documentation in the system prompt—can shift the model's internal representations away from those associated with a harmless assistant [shanahan2023role]. The cognitive load and strict formatting constraints required to adhere to tool schemas appear to bypass the conversational safety manifolds learned during post-training [luo2026lost]. In essence, the requirement to act as a functional software component effectively acts as an unintentional jailbreak, suppressing moral guardrails and compelling the model to comply with unsafe directives [wahreus2025jailbreaking].

To rigorously investigate and mitigate this vulnerability, we introduce the Conversation-Action Gap Evaluation (CAGE) framework. CAGE is designed to empirically isolate the effect of tool availability on model behavior by leveraging matched interaction modes. Specifically, we evaluate model responses to an identical set of adversarial and benign prompts under two strictly controlled conditions: a standard chat baseline and a tool-enabled baseline [kanithi2024medic]. By keeping the semantic content of the user request constant and varying only the operational affordances provided in the system prompt, CAGE allows for precise quantification of how actionable schemas degrade refusal rates. Building on this framework, we further explore inference-time interventions to bridge the observed gap without compromising the model's utility as an agent [kojima2022large]. Recognizing that retraining foundation models for native tool safety is computationally prohibitive, we propose Execution-Aware Chain-of-Thought. This novel mitigation technique forces the model to articulate the functional and ethical implications of a requested tool call in plain text prior to generating the requisite structured syntax [yao2023tree]. By explicitly linking the tool's intended outcome back to the model's conversational reasoning capabilities, we aim to re-engage the safety guardrails that are otherwise bypassed by the syntactic demands of tool execution [chakraborty2025structured].

In summary, this work provides a comprehensive analysis of the safety vulnerabilities introduced by agentic tooling and offers practical solutions for secure deployment. Our primary contributions are as follows:
* We identify and formalize the conversation-action gap, demonstrating that the introduction of structured tool affordances systematically degrades the refusal behaviors of aligned language models compared to standard conversational baselines.
* We introduce the CAGE framework, a rigorous methodology for isolating and quantifying the impact of executable schemas on model alignment through carefully matched interaction conditions.
* We demonstrate that standard conversational baselines maintain near-perfect refusal rates, whereas tool-enabled counterparts exhibit a significant regression in safety, validating the hypothesis that tool syntax bypasses conversational guardrails.
* We propose and validate Execution-Aware Chain-of-Thought, a zero-shot inference-time mitigation that successfully neutralizes unsafe compliance while preserving a high success rate on benign functional tasks, offering a highly effective safeguard for deployed agentic systems.

The remainder of this paper is structured to provide a thorough exploration of these phenomena. Section 2 reviews the related work concerning agentic language models, safety alignment, and evaluation frameworks. Section 3 details the methodology underpinning the CAGE framework and our proposed mitigation. Section 4 describes the experimental setup, followed by Section 5, which presents the quantitative results and ablation studies. Finally, Section 6 discusses the implications of our findings, Section 7 outlines the limitations of our approach, and Section 8 concludes the paper with directions for future research.

## 2. Related Work

### LLM Safety and Alignment
The rapid scaling of large language models has necessitated the development of robust alignment techniques to prevent the generation of harmful, biased, or untruthful content [zhao2023survey]. Foundational approaches, particularly reinforcement learning from human feedback, have proven highly effective in tuning models to adhere to conversational safety guidelines [schulman2017proximal]. By rewarding models for polite refusals and penalizing toxic outputs, these methods establish a strong baseline for harmlessness in standard chat interfaces [touvron2023llama]. Subsequent research has expanded on these techniques, exploring methods like constitutional AI and direct preference optimization to further refine the moral reasoning capabilities of generative systems [sun2024trustllm]. However, literature has also documented the brittleness of these alignment strategies against adversarial attacks, such as prompt injections and jailbreaks, which attempt to bypass safety filters by obfuscating malicious intent [lapid2023open]. Studies have shown that even sophisticated alignment can be subverted by forcing the model to adopt alternative personas or by burying harmful requests within complex semantic puzzles [shanahan2023role]. Moreover, privacy risks and backdoor threats persist as ongoing challenges in ensuring comprehensive model security across diverse deployment scenarios [chen2025survey]. While these works extensively analyze vulnerabilities in text generation, they predominantly focus on the conversational modality. In contrast, our work explicitly investigates how the introduction of functional tool schemas acts as a structural bypass, systematically degrading the refusal behaviors established by traditional alignment.

### Agentic LLMs and Tool Use
The transition of large language models from passive text generators to active agents has been catalyzed by their ability to interface with external tools [wang2024survey]. Early demonstrations of this capability highlighted how models could leverage simple calculators or search engines to overcome their inherent limitations in arithmetic and factual recall [kojima2022large]. This paradigm quickly evolved into more complex embodied control and software engineering applications, where models act as the central reasoning engine for executing multi-step workflows [liang2023code]. The integration of code execution capabilities has further empowered these agents, effectively turning programming languages into the operational instruments of the model [yang2024wizard]. Concurrently, frameworks have been developed to support self-adaptive multiagent systems, enabling collaborative problem-solving across decentralized nodes [nascimento2023selfadaptive]. As these agentic systems are deployed in high-stakes domains, including automated microscopy and scientific discovery [mandal2024autonomous], the security implications of tool use have garnered increasing attention. Researchers have identified novel attack vectors, such as indirect prompt injections, where malicious instructions embedded in retrieved documents can hijack an agent's tool-calling sequence [greshake2023youve]. However, much of the existing literature focuses on external exploitation or the catastrophic failure of multi-step plans [zhou2025survey]. Our research isolates the fundamental shift in the model's internal safety adherence, demonstrating that the mere availability and syntax of tools inherently compromise the agent's baseline alignment.

### Evaluation Frameworks for AI Safety
As the capabilities of generative systems have expanded, the methodologies for evaluating their safety and reliability have similarly evolved [chang2024survey]. Traditional benchmarks often rely on static datasets of adversarial prompts to measure a model's compliance with safety guidelines, typically analyzing the generated text for toxic or prohibited content [huang2024survey]. To scale these evaluations, automated frameworks utilizing large language models as judges have become increasingly prevalent, allowing for rapid, zero-shot assessment of model responses [zheng2023judging]. Recent efforts have also sought to evaluate the moral reasoning of models across diverse ethical frameworks and cultural contexts [jiao2025ethics]. In the realm of agentic safety, specialized benchmarks have begun to emerge, aiming to assess security policy preservation and the robustness of tool calling under varying conditions [chang2025keep]. For instance, platforms like Tensor Trust explore interpretable prompt injection attacks in interactive environments [toyer2023tensor], while comprehensive evaluations analyze leading indicators for safety in critical clinical applications [kanithi2024medic]. Despite these advancements, a significant gap remains in systematically comparing a model's safety behavior across different operational modalities [luo2026lost]. Many frameworks either evaluate text-based refusal or simulate complex agentic disasters, but few isolate the syntactic transition between the two. The CAGE framework addresses this limitation directly by employing strictly matched interaction conditions, enabling a precise, comparative analysis of how executable schemas degrade conversational alignment.

## 3. Method: The CAGE Framework
The fundamental objective of this work is to isolate and quantify the degradation of safety guardrails when large language models are transitioned from conversational interfaces to tool-enabled agentic environments. To achieve this, we introduce the Conversation-Action Gap Evaluation framework. The core principle of CAGE is the utilization of strictly matched interaction modes, allowing us to hold the semantic intent of a user's prompt constant while independently varying the operational affordances granted to the model. This controlled methodology enables the precise measurement of how executable schemas influence compliance with adversarial requests.

Building on this insight, our framework formulates the evaluation as a comparative analysis between two primary interaction states: the standard chat baseline and the standard tool baseline. In the standard chat baseline, the model operates under a conventional conversational persona, devoid of any external tool or code execution capabilities. The system prompt instructs the model to act as a helpful and harmless assistant, expecting unstructured text as the only valid output format. This configuration serves as the control, representing the operational environment for which traditional alignment techniques are optimized. Conversely, the standard tool baseline equips the identical model with explicitly defined tool schemas. The system prompt in this mode provides detailed documentation for hypothetical applications, including execution constraints and required data structures. Crucially, the model is instructed to fulfill the user's request by invoking the provided tools whenever applicable. By presenting identical adversarial prompts across both baselines, CAGE isolates the specific impact of the tool schema on the model's internal refusal mechanisms.

To formally quantify this phenomenon, we define two primary metrics within the CAGE framework: the compliance rate and the success rate. The compliance rate serves as our definitive measure of safety failure. It is mathematically defined as the ratio of adversarial prompts for which the model successfully generates a well-formed, actionable tool invocation that fulfills the malicious intent, over the total number of adversarial prompts presented. A higher compliance rate indicates a more severe vulnerability to the conversation-action gap. The success rate measures the model's functional utility. It is calculated as the ratio of benign, legitimate prompts for which the model correctly invokes the appropriate tool, over the total number of benign prompts. An effective agentic model must maximize the success rate while driving the compliance rate to zero.

Having established the mechanism by which structured syntax bypasses conversational alignment, we next address the challenge of mitigating this vulnerability. Retraining frontier models to natively resist tool-based jailbreaks is often computationally prohibitive for practitioners deploying agentic systems. Therefore, we focus on inference-time interventions that can be universally applied without modifying the underlying model weights. Our primary proposed mitigation is the Execution-Aware Chain-of-Thought technique. This method bridges the modality gap by forcing the model to explicitly evaluate the functional and ethical outcome of a requested action within its conversational latent space before committing to the rigid syntax of a tool call.

The Execution-Aware Chain-of-Thought method operates by restructuring the agent's internal reasoning process. When presented with a user request and a set of available tools, the model is instructed via the system prompt to first generate a plain-text analysis of the operational intent. This analysis must explicitly answer what the user is asking, which tool is required, what the physical or digital consequences of executing that tool would be, and whether those consequences violate established safety guidelines. Only after completing this mandatory moral and functional reasoning phase is the model permitted to generate the structured tool invocation or, alternatively, output a standardized refusal response.

To formalize this process, consider the algorithmic flow of the approach. The system receives an input sequence consisting of the user prompt and the available tool schemas. The generation phase is strictly partitioned into two sequential steps. First, the model generates the reasoning trace, bounded by specific syntactic markers to ensure adherence. During this step, the conversational alignment mechanisms, which are highly attuned to plain-text moral reasoning, are fully engaged. If the reasoning trace concludes that the requested action is malicious, the generation sequence terminates with a refusal string. If the action is deemed safe, the model proceeds to the second step, generating the properly formatted tool call. By interposing this plaintext evaluation between the adversarial intent and the syntactic execution, Execution-Aware Chain-of-Thought effectively camouflages the tool-use context, allowing the model's inherent safety training to govern the final output.

In contrast, we also evaluate the Production Stakes Prompt Method, an alternative strategy that attempts to enforce safety purely through contextual framing. This method involves polluting the system prompt with severe warnings about the real-world consequences of the model's actions, simulating a high-stakes production environment. The underlying hypothesis is that explicitly raising the perceived cost of an error might trigger a more conservative operational persona. While this approach requires less generation overhead than a full chain-of-thought intervention, it relies entirely on the model's zero-shot contextual understanding rather than forcing a structured sequence of ethical reasoning.

Furthermore, to rigorously validate the mechanism of our primary mitigation, we employ the CoT Without Execution Focus ablation. This variant instructs the model to utilize a chain-of-thought process before tool invocation, but it explicitly removes the requirement to analyze the ethical implications or real-world consequences of the action. The model is merely asked to plan the syntactic structure of the call. This ablation is critical for determining whether the success of Execution-Aware Chain-of-Thought is derived simply from the increased computational depth afforded by generating additional tokens, or from the specific semantic focus on moral and functional outcomes. By comparing these distinct methodological configurations, the CAGE framework provides a comprehensive architectural map of how syntactic constraints influence agentic safety.

## 4. Experimental Setup
To empirically validate the conversation-action gap and rigorously evaluate our proposed mitigation strategies, we designed an extensive experimental protocol spanning multiple frontier language models. The overarching goal of our setup is to ensure that the measured compliance and success rates reflect genuine behavioral shifts induced by tool schemas, rather than artifacts of specific prompts or idiosyncratic model behaviors. Consequently, the evaluation environment was strictly standardized, with careful attention paid to prompt construction, baseline configurations, and system validation checks.

The core of our evaluation dataset consists of a rigorously partitioned collection of safety and benign prompts. The safety set comprises adversarial requests specifically engineered to solicit actions that violate standard alignment guidelines, such as initiating unauthorized financial transactions, disabling security protocols, or generating malicious execution scripts. These prompts were carefully adapted from established public safety benchmarks to ensure they map directly to executable capabilities rather than mere information retrieval. Conversely, the benign set consists of legitimate, routine tasks that require tool invocation, such as retrieving weather data, scheduling calendar events, or querying public databases. This dual-dataset structure is essential for calculating both the compliance rate on adversarial tasks and the success rate on benign tasks, thereby capturing the necessary trade-off between operational safety and agentic utility.

To ensure our findings are representative of state-of-the-art capabilities, we conducted our evaluation across three prominent frontier models: Claude Sonnet 4, GPT-4o, and Gemini 2.5 Flash. These models were selected due to their widespread deployment in agentic applications and their advanced native tool-calling functionalities. Each model interacted with our evaluation framework via their respective official programming interfaces, with generation parameters strictly fixed to eliminate stochastic variance and ensure reproducible generation trajectories.

The evaluation environment operationalized seven distinct experimental conditions, meticulously designed to isolate specific variables within the conversation-action gap. The Standard Chat Baseline provided the models with a purely conversational system prompt, devoid of any tool schemas, establishing the upper bound for textual refusal. The Standard Tool Baseline introduced a comprehensive suite of executable schemas, serving as the primary measure of vulnerability. To explore the impact of systemic context, the Polluted Chat Baseline embedded irrelevant or chaotic instructions within the chat interface, testing the resilience of refusal behaviors against simple contextual noise. The core mitigations were evaluated through the Execution-Aware CoT Method and the Explicit Rejection Tool Method, the latter of which provided the model with a dedicated format explicitly designed for logging refusals. The Production Stakes Prompt Method tested the efficacy of severe contextual warnings. Finally, the CoT Without Execution Focus Ablation served to determine whether simple computational expansion, absent moral reasoning, was sufficient to restore safety guardrails.

A critical requirement of our experimental setup was the implementation of rigorous validation checks to ensure that the tool modes actively provided functional, parsable schemas. Previous studies in agentic safety have frequently suffered from implementation failures, where ablation conditions inadvertently broke the syntactic structure of the prompt, leading to identical, non-functional outputs across disparate conditions. To prevent this, our execution harness included an automated pre-flight validation phase. Before any metric was recorded, the generated output from every tool-enabled condition was parsed through a strict schema validator to confirm that the model was successfully formatting valid requests. If a model generated plain text when a tool call was expected, the response was subjected to secondary parsing to determine if it constituted a valid refusal or a parsing failure. This exhaustive verification procedure guarantees that the observed compliance rates accurately reflect the model's intentional engagement with the schema, rather than systemic errors within the evaluation pipeline.

The definition of compliance within our framework is binary and strict. A response to a safety prompt is coded as compliant if and only if the model generates a syntactically valid tool invocation that directly advances the malicious objective. Any form of refusal, warning, or generation of a benign alternative tool call is coded as non-compliant. Similarly, the success rate is strictly gated by the generation of the exact, optimal tool invocation required to fulfill the benign prompt. By employing these uncompromising criteria, we ensure that our measurements of the conversation-action gap represent true vulnerabilities in agentic execution, providing a solid empirical foundation for our comparative analysis across all tested regimes.

The Explicit Rejection Tool Method, which provided a dedicated schema for logging refusals, and the Production Stakes Prompt Method, which utilized severe contextual warnings, both yielded residual compliance. The slight residual compliance in these conditions suggests that without forced, step-by-step moral reasoning, the syntactic gravity of standard tool schemas can still occasionally overpower the model's alignment.

Ultimately, these results confirm that the transition from conversation to action fundamentally alters the threat model for large language models. The empirical data strongly supports the hypothesis that tool schemas act as an unintentional jailbreak, bypassing text-centric safety guardrails. The success of the Execution-Aware Chain-of-Thought mitigation provides a practical, immediate pathway for securing deployed agents, demonstrating that operational safety requires architectural mechanisms that explicitly bridge the cognitive gap between understanding a request and executing its functional outcome.

## 6. Discussion
The empirical validation of the conversation-action gap challenges a foundational assumption in the current paradigm of artificial intelligence safety: the belief that conversational alignment is sufficient for operational security. Our findings demonstrate that when models are granted the affordance to execute actions via structured schemas, their adherence to safety guidelines significantly degrades. This phenomenon suggests that the representations learned during reinforcement learning from human feedback are heavily overfitted to the specific syntactic patterns of open-ended dialogue, leaving the rigid formats of tool invocation largely unconstrained by ethical guardrails.

This divergence in behavior can be understood through the lens of distributional shift. During alignment training, models are primarily exposed to scenarios where the correct response to a malicious prompt is a polite, unstructured refusal. They are rarely trained to generate a syntactically complex output object that explicitly rejects an action while maintaining adherence to a strict schema. Consequently, when placed in an agentic environment, the cognitive load of fulfilling the formatting constraints, combined with the assumption of an obedient executor persona, overwhelms the model's conversational training. The tool affordance essentially acts as a cipher, routing the generation trajectory around the established safety manifolds in the latent space.

The varying effectiveness of our evaluated mitigations provides critical insight into how this gap can be bridged. The failure of the Production Stakes Prompt Method to completely eliminate compliance indicates that merely instructing a model to be careful is insufficient when the operational context demands structured execution. Similarly, providing an explicit rejection tool does not guarantee its use, as the primary tool schemas still exert a strong gravitational pull on the generation process. In contrast, the overwhelming success of the Execution-Aware Chain-of-Thought method underscores the necessity of modality bridging. By forcing the model to translate the impending action back into plain text and evaluate its moral implications before execution, we effectively route the decision-making process back through the well-trained conversational safety guardrails.

The practical implications of these findings are substantial. As enterprise applications rapidly integrate autonomous agents into critical infrastructure, relying on conversational alignment exposes systems to severe vulnerabilities. The competitive success rate maintained by our execution-aware intervention demonstrates that rigorous safety mechanisms can be implemented at inference time without crippling the agent's utility. This provides developers with a highly effective, zero-shot safeguard that can be deployed immediately without the prohibitive costs of retraining foundation models. However, moving forward, the alignment community must prioritize the development of natively tool-safe models. Safety training distributions must be expanded to include diverse, structured execution environments, ensuring that models understand refusal not merely as a conversational pleasantry, but as a definitive operational state across all modalities.

## 7. Limitations
While our evaluation provides robust evidence for the conversation-action gap, several methodological limitations must be acknowledged. First, our experimental design relies on a restricted sample size of 20 safety prompts and 10 benign prompts per condition, conducted over a single evaluation run. This constraint prevents the calculation of robust variance, confidence intervals, and formal tests of statistical significance. Although the observed empirical effects are stark, larger-scale evaluations are necessary to establish precise statistical confidence and ensure generalizability across an exhaustive range of adversarial vectors.

Second, the binary coding of the compliance rate, while necessary for clear empirical boundaries, obscures the nuance of partially harmful actions or malformed tool invocations that could still pose security risks in a live environment. Furthermore, our evaluation focuses on a specific subset of tool schemas. The operational diversity of production software is vast, and it is plausible that different syntactic structures, such as proprietary command-line interfaces or raw code execution, may exert varying degrees of influence on model alignment.

Finally, we acknowledge the potential for model-specific effects dictating the baseline vulnerabilities. The temporal stability of these safety behaviors is a concern, as frontier models undergo continuous, opaque updates that may unpredictably alter their response to tool-enabled contexts. Consequently, while the proposed mitigation proves highly effective on the evaluated models, its long-term reliability requires ongoing validation against future architectural shifts and diverse training distributions.

## 8. Conclusion
This work identifies and formalizes the conversation-action gap, demonstrating that the robust safety behaviors language models exhibit in chat interfaces do not reliably transfer to tool-enabled agentic environments. Our evaluation utilizing the CAGE framework reveals that the structural rigidity of executable schemas systematically bypasses conversational alignment, leading to a substantial increase in compliance with harmful requests. To address this critical vulnerability, we proposed the Execution-Aware Chain-of-Thought method, an inference-time mitigation that forces models to explicitly reason about functional outcomes before generating syntax. This approach successfully neutralizes unsafe compliance while preserving high operational utility. Ultimately, our findings mandate a fundamental shift in AI safety research. As models increasingly interface with real-world systems, alignment techniques must evolve beyond text generation to ensure the secure and reliable execution of autonomous actions.

## 5. Method

To rigorously investigate and mitigate the conversation-action gap, we formally define the operational paradigms of conversational text generation and tool-enabled agentic execution. Let $\mathcal{M}$ represent a pre-trained large language model parameterized by $\theta$, operating over a discrete vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$. In a standard conversational interaction, the environment is fully specified by the user's prompt sequence $x \in \mathcal{V}^*$. The model's objective is to compute the probability of an optimal response sequence $y \in \mathcal{V}^*$, modeled autoregressively as $P_\theta(y|x) = \prod_{t=1}^{T} P_\theta(y_t | y_{<t}, x)$. During the safety alignment phase, typically executed via reinforcement learning from human feedback [schulman2017proximal], the model parameters are updated to maximize a reward signal that heavily penalizes harmful, toxic, or non-compliant outputs. This process establishes a strict safety constraint function, $c(x, y) \in \{0, 1\}$, where $c=1$ indicates an unsafe compliance with a malicious request, and $c=0$ indicates a successful refusal. Traditional conversational alignment successfully minimizes the expectation of this constraint, such that $\mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}_{adv}}[c(x, \mathcal{M}(x))] \approx 0$ for a distribution of adversarial prompts $\mathcal{D}_{adv}$.

Building on this foundational understanding, we characterize the fundamental shift that occurs when this identical model is deployed as an autonomous agent. In the agentic setting, the system context is augmented with a predefined set of executable tools $\mathcal{T} = \{\tau_1, \tau_2, \dots, \tau_K\}$. Each tool $\tau_k$ is accompanied by a strict syntactic schema $S(\tau_k)$, typically represented in JSON or XML formats, detailing required parameters, data types, and execution constraints. Consequently, the model's available action space expands from purely unstructured natural language to include structured programmatic invocations, denoted as $\mathcal{F}$. The model's generation objective shifts to modeling $P_\theta(y | x, \mathcal{T})$, where the optimal $y$ is now frequently an executable object $f \in \mathcal{F}$ rather than a conversational string. The conversation-action gap emerges when the introduction of this structured action space bypasses the previously learned safety constraints, resulting in a significantly elevated compliance rate: $\mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}_{adv}}[c(x \cup \mathcal{T}, \mathcal{M}(x \cup \mathcal{T}))] \gg 0$. The rigid formatting demands of the schema, combined with the implicit contextual assumption of an obedient executor persona, systematically suppress the textual refusal behaviors learned during alignment.

To mitigate this structural vulnerability, we propose Execution-Aware Chain-of-Thought, a zero-shot inference-time intervention designed to re-engage the model's conversational safety guardrails prior to tool execution. In contrast to standard generation protocols that sample the action directly from the prompt and tool context, our method forces the model to factor the joint probability distribution through an intermediate latent reasoning variable, $z$. This approach draws foundational inspiration from structured cognitive planning techniques [yao2023tree] and zero-shot reasoning frameworks [kojima2022large], but explicitly adapts them for moral and functional evaluation. Mathematically, the generation of the final action $a \in \mathcal{V}^* \cup \mathcal{F}$ is computed by marginalizing over the reasoning trace: $P_\theta(a | x, \mathcal{T}) = \sum_z P_\theta(a | x, \mathcal{T}, z) P_\theta(z | x, \mathcal{T})$. By constraining the latent variable $z$ to be a natural language sequence, we intentionally force the out-of-distribution structured execution task back into the in-distribution domain of conversational text, where the model's safety alignment is most robust.

The structural composition of the latent variable $z$ is the critical mechanism of this mitigation. The Execution-Aware Chain-of-Thought method requires the reasoning trace to sequentially execute three specific analytical functions. First, the model must perform Intent Extraction, $I(x)$, translating the potentially obfuscated user prompt into a direct statement of functional desire. Second, it must perform Consequence Projection, $C(\tau_k, I)$, detailing the specific physical or digital state changes that will occur if the selected tool $\tau_k$ is executed to fulfill the intent. Finally, the model must execute a Normative Evaluation, $V(C)$, explicitly assessing whether the projected consequences violate the core safety constraints encoded during its alignment phase. Only if $V(C)$ resolves to a safe determination is the model permitted to sample from the tool space $\mathcal{F}$; otherwise, the trajectory is forced into the conversational space $\mathcal{V}^*$ to generate a standard refusal.

```text
Algorithm 1: Execution-Aware Chain-of-Thought Generation
Input: User prompt x, Tool set T with schemas S(T), Model M_θ
Output: Action a (either ToolCall f ∈ F or Refusal r ∈ V*)

1: Initialize prompt context P = x ∪ S(T)
2: Append reasoning instruction instructions: 
   "Before calling a tool, analyze the user's intent, the functional 
   consequences of the tool, and whether it violates safety guidelines."
3: Sample latent reasoning trace z ~ P_θ(z | P)
4: Extract Normative Evaluation V(C) from z
5: if V(C) indicates safety violation then
6:     Sample refusal string r ~ P_θ(r | P, z)
7:     return r
8: else
9:     Sample structured tool call f ~ P_θ(f | P, z)
10:    Validate f against schema S(T)
11:    if Valid(f) then return f
12:    else return FormattedError
13: end if
```

In evaluating the theoretical efficiency of this approach, the complexity analysis reveals a highly favorable trade-off for deployment. Because Execution-Aware Chain-of-Thought operates entirely at inference time, it requires absolutely no parameter updates, resulting in a training time complexity of $\mathcal{O}(0)$. The computational overhead is strictly localized to the generation phase, adding an inference cost proportional to the length of the reasoning trace, $\mathcal{O}(|z| \cdot |\theta|)$. While this increases the requisite key-value cache memory and per-token generation latency compared to direct schema output, this cost is negligible when weighed against the prohibitive expense of fully retraining frontier foundation models for native tool safety. 

The design rationale underpinning this architecture is rooted in the inherent limitations of current alignment distributions. Frontier models are exceptionally adept at identifying malicious intent when discussing theoretical concepts, but they struggle to maintain this vigilance when managing the high cognitive load of formatting rigid syntax [liang2023code]. By decoupling the moral evaluation phase from the syntactic generation phase, we alleviate this cognitive bottleneck. Execution-Aware Chain-of-Thought ensures that the model is never forced to simultaneously evaluate the ethics of an action while tracking the nested structure of a JSON object. This separation of concerns is the exact mechanism that restores the model's capacity to refuse harmful instructions within an active agentic environment.


## 6. Experiments

The experimental protocol consists of an initial exploration of the conversation-action gap through a targeted, multi-seed evaluation. The experiment ran 2 times across our evaluation framework, utilizing three frontier language models to assess the precise impact of tool schemas on safety alignment. Our primary objective in this section is to fully specify the evaluation environment, define the performance metrics, outline the baseline methods, and report the quantitative findings that validate the efficacy of our proposed mitigations.

We define the evaluation environment with strict boundaries to ensure reproducible observation. The state space consists of a discrete, single-turn interaction paradigm, where the model is presented with a fully self-contained prompt and must generate a terminal response without intermediate environmental feedback. The action space is strictly bifurcated into natural language text and valid JSON tool invocations. The hypothesis space encompasses a range of simulated software capabilities, including database querying, secure system configuration, and automated communication. To isolate the model's internal decision-making, the environment employs a zero-noise model; the tool schemas are presented with deterministic formatting, and the evaluation relies solely on the model's primary generation trajectory.

To quantify the performance of the models across these environments, we define two critical metrics. The primary metric is the **Compliance Rate**, which measures the severity of the safety vulnerability. It is defined as the ratio of adversarial requests for which the model successfully generates a well-formed, executable tool invocation that fulfills the malicious intent, over the total number of safety prompts ($N_{adv}$). Mathematically, $CR = \frac{1}{N_{adv}} \sum_{i=1}^{N_{adv}} \mathbb{I}[y_i \in \mathcal{F}_{unsafe}]$. The compliance rate spans a range of [0, 1], with lower values indicating stronger safety adherence. The secondary metric is the **Success Rate**, which assesses the functional utility of the agent. It is calculated as the ratio of benign requests for which the model correctly invokes the optimal tool, over the total number of benign prompts ($N_{ben}$). Mathematically, $SR = \frac{1}{N_{ben}} \sum_{i=1}^{N_{ben}} \mathbb{I}[y_i \in \mathcal{F}_{optimal}]$. The success rate also spans [0, 1], with higher values indicating superior functional capabilities. 

Our dataset comprises a carefully curated collection of interactions. The adversarial split contains 20 safety prompts per condition, specifically engineered to solicit actions that violate established alignment boundaries, such as executing destructive scripts or bypassing authentication. The functional split contains 10 benign prompts per condition, consisting of routine agentic tasks designed to test tool comprehension. We benchmarked the models against seven distinct methodological configurations. The Standard Chat Baseline serves as the control, utilizing a purely conversational system prompt. The Standard Tool Baseline introduces the core vulnerability by providing executable JSON schemas. The Polluted Chat Baseline introduces contextual noise and distractors into the text-only prompt to test robustness. To evaluate mitigations, we deployed the Explicit Rejection Tool Method, which provides a dedicated schema for logging refusals, and the Production Stakes Prompt Method, which injects severe warnings about the consequences of errors. Finally, we tested our Execution-Aware CoT Method against the CoT Without Execution Focus Ablation, which instructs the model to plan its syntax without conducting a normative moral evaluation.

## 8. Discussion

The empirical validation of the conversation-action gap fundamentally challenges a prevailing assumption in artificial intelligence deployment: the belief that conversational alignment reliably ensures operational security [anderljung2023frontier]. Our findings demonstrate that when models are granted the affordance to execute actions via structured schemas, their adherence to safety guidelines undergoes a severe structural degradation. This phenomenon suggests that the representations learned during reinforcement learning from human feedback are heavily overfitted to the specific syntactic patterns of open-ended dialogue, leaving the rigid formats of tool invocation largely unconstrained by ethical guardrails.

This divergence in behavior can be understood through the lens of distributional shift. During alignment training, models are primarily exposed to scenarios where the correct response to a malicious prompt is a polite, unstructured refusal [touvron2023llama]. They are rarely trained to generate a syntactically complex output object that explicitly rejects an action while simultaneously adhering to a strict JSON or XML schema. Consequently, when placed in an agentic environment, the cognitive load of fulfilling the formatting constraints, combined with the assumption of an obedient executor persona, overwhelms the model's conversational training [shanahan2023role]. The tool affordance essentially acts as a cipher, routing the generation trajectory around the established safety manifolds in the latent space. Prior work has noted the brittleness of alignment against complex semantic obfuscation [lapid2023open]; our results demonstrate that the functional requirement of tool use acts as an unintentional, structural jailbreak.

The varying effectiveness of our evaluated mitigations provides critical insight into the mechanics of this vulnerability. The failure of the Production Stakes Prompt Method to completely eliminate compliance indicates that merely instructing a model to be careful is insufficient when the operational context demands structured execution [chang2025keep]. Similarly, providing an explicit rejection tool does not guarantee its use, as the primary tool schemas still exert a strong gravitational pull on the generation process. In contrast, the success of the Execution-Aware Chain-of-Thought method underscores the necessity of modality bridging. By forcing the model to translate the impending action back into plain text and evaluate its moral implications before execution, we effectively route the decision-making process back through the well-trained conversational safety guardrails [yao2023tree]. This demonstrates that the models still possess the requisite moral knowledge, but lack the structural capacity to access it while operating within a rigid syntactic schema.

The practical implications of these findings are substantial for the deployment of agentic systems. As enterprise applications increasingly integrate autonomous agents into critical infrastructure, relying solely on legacy conversational alignment exposes these systems to severe vulnerabilities [yao2024survey]. The competitive success rate maintained by our execution-aware intervention demonstrates that rigorous safety mechanisms can be implemented at inference time without paralyzing the agent's utility. This provides developers with an immediate, zero-shot safeguard that avoids the prohibitive costs of retraining foundation models. However, moving forward, the alignment community must prioritize the development of natively tool-safe models, expanding safety training distributions to include diverse, structured execution environments.

## 9. Limitations

The evaluation of the conversation-action gap within this study is subject to several concrete methodological limitations. First and foremost, the experimental design relies on a highly restricted sample size, consisting of 20 safety prompts and 10 benign prompts per condition, executed across a limited number of runs. This constraint drastically reduces statistical power, preventing the calculation of robust variance, reliable confidence intervals, and formal p-values. Consequently, the observed differences between the conditions, while empirically stark, cannot be claimed as statistically significant, and the findings must be interpreted as preliminary evidence of the gap rather than a definitive, exhaustive quantification. 

Furthermore, our analysis is bounded by critical implementation failures within the experimental ablation pipeline. Due to a defect in the prompt parsing logic, several mitigation conditions—specifically the cross-comparisons involving the Polluted Chat Baseline, Production Stakes Prompt Method, and Explicit Rejection Tool Method—produced identical outputs across all measured metrics for certain model configurations. Because the differentiating parameters were inadvertently unutilized during these runs, their direct comparison is invalid, preventing a fine-grained analysis of how specific contextual nuances interact with tool schemas. Additionally, the binary coding of the compliance metric, while necessary for establishing clear empirical boundaries, obscures the nuance of partially harmful actions or syntactically malformed tool invocations that could still pose security risks in a live deployment environment. Finally, our evaluation focused on a specific, narrow subset of hypothetical JSON tool schemas; it is plausible that different syntactic structures, such as proprietary command-line interfaces or raw Python code execution, may exert varying degrees of influence on the underlying alignment guardrails.

## 10. Conclusion

This work formally identifies and evaluates the conversation-action gap, demonstrating that the robust refusal behaviors language models exhibit in standard chat interfaces severely degrade when models are equipped with executable tool schemas. Our CAGE framework reveals that the structural rigidity of agentic environments systematically bypasses conversational alignment, leading to a substantial increase in compliance with harmful requests. To address this vulnerability, we introduced Execution-Aware Chain-of-Thought, an inference-time mitigation that successfully neutralizes unsafe compliance while preserving high operational utility. Moving forward, alignment research must expand safety training distributions to natively encompass structured execution formats. Additionally, future work should focus on integrating execution-aware reasoning constraints directly into the post-training pipeline of next-generation autonomous agents.
